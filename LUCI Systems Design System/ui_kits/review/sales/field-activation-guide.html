LUCI Systems · Field Activation Guide 02

The principle

One floor. One platform. One strategy.

Before LUCI, most casino and resort teams worked the floor separately. Marketing ran the signage, Operations shaped the gaming environment, A/V kept the screens and sound running — each in its own lane, on its own equipment, with few ways to coordinate.

Now every screen, zone, and audio channel moves on a single platform. Marketing, Gaming and Operations, A/V and Facilities, and IT aren’t working separate equipment that happens to share a building. They’re working the same floor, through the same platform, toward the same guest.

That’s the real opportunity. When your teams decide together how the floor should work — which messages run where, how energy builds through the day, what happens when a jackpot hits or an event lets out — they’re building one intentional guest-experience strategy. Set the plan together, and each team can move fast in its own lane, because everyone knows how the pieces fit. Real-time changes still happen; they just happen against a shared plan.

That’s how this guide is built. The pages ahead are organized by team — Marketing, Gaming & Operations, A/V & Facilities, IT, Finance, and General Management. Each section gathers the ways that team can put LUCI to work on the floor: four use cases, each with one task to try this week. Turn to your team’s section, or read straight through to see how the whole floor fits together.

Plan the floor together. Then activate it.

Marketing

Every screen on the property is now yours to program — by zone, by daypart, in real time. The reach that used to live on a handful of dedicated signs now covers the whole floor.

The right message, in the right zone, at the right moment becomes a lever you control — to extend time on device, lift on-property spend, and drive trip frequency.

  • 01

    Push a message to every screen in under two minutes.

    A promotion changes, an event is cancelled, or the weather turns and you want to pull guests toward indoor amenities. You make the edit on your own device, send it to the whole floor or a single set of zones, and it’s live in moments — then drop back to the scheduled plan when you’re done.

    • React while the moment still matters, instead of after it passes
    • No ticket, no call, no waiting on another team’s queue
    • Every screen stays current — none stranded on yesterday’s message

    Try it →Find one piece of content that needed outside help to change last time. Change it yourself today.

  • 02

    Daypart your restaurant promotions to catch guests at the decision point.

    Schedule a dining spot to run across the floor in the hour before lunch and again before dinner — the windows when guests are deciding whether to stay on property or head out — then hand back to the regular rotation automatically. Set the timing once and it repeats every day.

    • Capture covers that would otherwise walk out the door
    • Reach guests anywhere on the floor, not just the screens by the restaurants
    • Extend time on device and lift on-property spend in the same move

    Try it →Build a pre-lunch dining spot for this week. Stand on the floor at 11am, watch it run, then hand back on its own.

Gaming & Operations

The floor’s environment is now a single instrument you can play — audio, lighting, and screens together. The energy guests feel is something you set on purpose, by zone and by hour, instead of leaving to chance.

A floor that responds — to the time of day, to a jackpot, to the room you actually have — keeps players engaged and the energy where you want it.

  • 01

    Set your floor’s audio to match the room’s energy, all day.

    Build an audio schedule that moves the way your floor does — lighter in the morning, building through the afternoon, peaking in the evening, easing back after the late-night crowd thins. Each zone follows its own curve, and the transitions happen on their own.

    • Keep the room feeling alive without a manager riding the faders
    • Match energy to the crowd you actually have at each hour
    • Give every shift the same intended sound, not whoever set it last

    Try it →Map your ideal audio curve for a weekday and build it as a scheduled preset.

  • 02

    Make every jackpot a floor event, automatically.

    When a jackpot hits, a coordinated preset fires in that zone — accent lights pulse, audio swells for a beat, nearby screens flash a celebration — then everything resets on its own. The response is calibrated per area so it fits the space.

    • Turn one guest’s win into energy the whole floor feels
    • Create a sense of action that keeps nearby players engaged
    • Deliver it every time, with no staff watching for the moment

    Try it →Work with A/V to build a jackpot preset for one zone, then test it in a low-traffic window.

    Build one preset for every mode you run, and switch in a single tap.

    Each mode your property runs — normal weekday, Saturday peak, fight night, holiday, a private event in the ballroom — becomes a complete configuration of audio, screens, and lighting. Some run on a tap; others, like the high-limit room, hold a standing identity that never needs a manual touch.

    • Reconfigure the entire property in seconds, not a shift of setup
    • Run premium spaces consistently no matter who’s working
    • Let predictable zones open and close themselves on schedule

    Try it →Identify the three or four modes you run most often and build a preset for each.

  • 04

    Direct guest attention to a new game area from the moment it opens.

    When a new slot bank or table section launches, set the surrounding zones to point toward it — ambient content on nearby screens, audio tuned to give the area its own energy — for the first few weeks, then revert automatically once it’s established.

    • Drive traffic to new product while it most needs the lift
    • Make the floor itself part of the launch, not just signage
    • Hand the spotlight back automatically when the window closes

    Try it →Plan the A/V component of your next new-game launch before the hardware arrives.

A/V & Facilities

The whole property’s audio and visual environment runs from one interface — every display, source, and zone, on or off site. The walk to a rack or a manual reset is the exception now, not the routine.

Build it once, then manage by exception. The floor holds its baseline on its own, and your day shifts from execution to oversight.

  • 01

    Build the whole property’s schedule in one session, then let it run.

    Spend a few hours configuring audio, displays, and zone behavior for every daypart, weekday and weekend, from one place. Once it’s built it runs itself, with a locked baseline underneath so the floor stays consistent no matter who opened — and you update any piece in real time without taking the rest offline.

    • Trade daily execution for oversight and the occasional adjustment
    • Eliminate the drift that creeps in shift to shift
    • Change one thing without disturbing anything else

    Try it →Block two hours to build a full seven-day audio schedule for your highest-traffic zones.

  • 02

    Resolve service requests from wherever you are — including after hours.

    A manager flags that the bar audio is off; you open that zone from your phone, adjust the level, and confirm it’s right — on property or off. Physical presence is reserved for genuinely physical problems, and every change logs itself.

    • Close out most issues in minutes, without a walk or a drive
    • Know whether a call really needs an on-site trip before anyone makes it
    • Keep an automatic record without a separate documentation step

    Try it →Next time a level complaint comes in, resolve it remotely and note how long it takes.

    • Guarantee the floor returns to normal the moment it’s over

    Try it →Build a preset for your most common event type and test it before the next one.

    See every A/V endpoint on the property from a single dashboard.

    Every connected display, audio component, and device appears in one interface with its current status and health, down to device-level detail — no trip to the server room and no call to a specialist to find out what’s going on.

    • Catch issues before they reach a help-desk ticket
    • Investigate with real data instead of guesswork
    • Treat A/V like any other managed part of the network

    Try it →Pull a full system health view this week and note how much is visible that wasn’t before.

  • 02

    Know exactly what changed, and when.

    Every configuration change is logged with who made it, what changed, and when — a complete audit trail you can filter by date, user, or zone to isolate exactly what happened before a problem appeared.

    • Trace any issue back to the change that caused it
    • Hold access accountable across every team
    • Confirm scheduled changes actually executed

    Try it →Review the change log from the past thirty days and note anything that surprises you.

Finance

A/V moves from a capital line to a predictable platform investment — and, for the first time, becomes something you can measure. The cost structure your old A/V budget was built around no longer applies.

Replan the capital, recover the contract spend, and start measuring the return. Each of these is a concrete budget action, not a concept.

  • 01

    Replan the capital you’d reserved for the next A/V refresh.

    The proprietary hardware that needed a full refresh every five to seven years is replaced by a software platform on a predictable annual line. Capability now arrives through updates rather than capital purchases, which changes the refresh request that used to drive your A/V budget.

    • Free the capital you’d set aside for the next hardware cycle
    • Trade a lumpy refresh expense for a predictable annual line
    • Forecast A/V spend instead of bracing for the next big ask

    Do this →Work with your FDE team to model the new refresh timeline and reallocate the capital you’d set aside.

  • 02

    Cut or renegotiate the vendor and integration contracts LUCI replaces.

    A large share of A/V’s old operating cost lived outside the property, in vendor and integration contracts that existed because the expertise didn’t. That control is internal now, so the contracts written around the old limitations are yours to consolidate or end.

    • Recover spend on scopes your team now handles directly
    • Consolidate overlapping vendors into one managed relationship
    • Retire contracts built around limitations that no longer exist

    Do this →Pull your current A/V vendor and integration contracts and renegotiate or cut what’s now redundant.

General Management

The whole guest experience is now something you can see and direct — from any device, in real time. Your job shifts from hoping the floor runs right to making sure your teams are using what they have.

The value compounds when every function turns it on. Your role is to set the floor strategy, keep it consistent, and make sure no team leaves capability on the table.

  • 01

    Walk the floor with an iPad and take the controls yourself.

    LUCI runs from any device. On your next walkthrough, open the dashboard and make a live change — flip a screen, adjust a zone’s audio, switch a preset — from wherever you’re standing, and confirm that what the platform reports matches what you see in person.

    • See and shape the guest experience without leaving the floor
    • Verify the floor is running the way it’s supposed to, live
    • Build your own fluency instead of relying on one operator

    Do this →Bring an iPad on your next walkthrough and test a live change; have your FDE show you the basics.

  • 02

    Schedule a morning reset so the floor always wakes up looking right.

    A TV gets flipped to local news, a screen gets left on the wrong input — it happens. A scheduled reset returns every display to its assigned content before the floor opens each day, with no one checking and nothing left to chance.

    • Open every day to a floor that’s already correct
    • Erase last night’s stray changes automatically
    • Free your team from a manual open-of-day checklist

    Do this →Ask your A/V team whether a morning reset is scheduled. If it isn’t, have them build one this week.
